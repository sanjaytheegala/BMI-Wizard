What is BMI?

Big Macs Inside? Belly Might Implode? Good guesses, but BMI actually stands for Body Mass Index, and itâ€™s a really important measure for anyone trying to lose weight. BMI is a simple numerical measure of your weight in relation to your height, it evaluates an individual's weight status based on their height and weight. Calculated by dividing one's weight in kilograms by the square of their height in meters, BMI serves as a screening tool for assessing weight status in adults.

BMI is used differently for people aged 2 to 20. It is calculated in the same way as for adults but then compared to typical values for other children or youth of the same age. Instead of comparison against fixed thresholds for underweight and overweight, the BMI is compared against the percentiles for children of the same sex and age. A BMI that is less than the 5th percentile is considered underweight and above the 95th percentile is considered obese. Children with a BMI between the 85th and 95th percentile are considered to be overweight.

Understanding BMI Categories

Once calculated, BMI values fall into different categories:

  1. Underweight: BMI below 18.5 indicates that a person may be underweight, which can pose health risks such as weakened immune system, osteoporosis, and fertility issues.

  2. Normal weight: BMI ranging from 18.5 to 24.9 suggests a healthy weight range for most adults. However,it's important to note that BMI does not differentiate between muscle mass and fat mass, so individuals with high muscle mass may fall into this category despite being fit.

  3. Overweight: BMI between 25 and 29.9 indicates excess weight, which may increase the risk of developing various health conditions including heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers.

  4. Obesity: BMI of 30 or higher signifies obesity, a condition associated with significantly increased health risks, including hypertension, stroke, sleep apnea, and joint problems.

  5. Extreme Obesity: BMI of 35 or higher is classified as extreme obesity. Individuals in this category face even greater health risks, including severe cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and reduced life expectancy.
